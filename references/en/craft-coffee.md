# Craft / signature coffee index (bloggers & shops)

> Iron rule: This file is only a "blogger/shop search index." Any specific recipe (ratio, ingredient grams, brew params) MUST be re-verified online against the shop/blogger's current public version before you write it into a reply, with a source link + retrieval date.
> Craft drinks iterate fast and are often seasonal; shop menus change often. Always defer to the current shop menu/official account.

## 1. What is craft / signature coffee

- A "coffee base" (espresso / pour-over / cold brew) layered with flavor ingredients and presentation to form a shop/blogger's signature.
- Two main lines:
  - **Milk/espresso craft**: latte / flat white / Dirty + flavors (cocoa, nut, fruit puree, floral).
  - **Clear/sparkling/cold-brew craft**: cold brew + juice / sparkling water / tea / herbs.

## 4. Espresso craft universal points (read before searching)

- Beans: milk craft uses medium-dark blend (sweet, thick, tames bitterness); bean-forward (SOE flat white, craft) uses light-roast SOE. See [recipes-baseline.md](recipes-baseline.md) section 9.
- Ingredient order: syrup/fruit puree first, espresso next, then milk/foam/ice — to avoid breaking the crema.
- Iced: weigh ice; hot extraction over ice dilutes instantly, especially in craft drinks.
- Balance: when ingredients add sweetness, the base espresso should be stronger (e.g. ristretto pull) to avoid cloying.
- Presentation: layers come from density difference (syrup bottom, milk middle, foam top); Dirty is the classic "no-stir" layer (milk below, espresso gently on top).

## 7. Common pitfalls

- Don't copy a shop recipe without asking about the beans — shop blends are custom; swap to a similar roast at home.
- Don't let heavy ingredients bury the coffee — craft is about balance; the coffee should still be there, not sugar water.
- Don't fabricate grams/ratios from a name — every number must come from online verification.
- Don't blur seasonal vs permanent — many signatures are seasonal; label "as of (date) menu."
- Do copy the framework (base + ingredient category) first, then tune ratios to your palate.
